Introduction

This book collects together the basic concepts of enriched category theory. We start from the elementary definitions and end by describing the categories of algebras for limit-defined theories. In general we introduce the ordinary and enriched theory simultaneously.

To define enriched categories we first define symmetric monoidal categories. If $\mathcal V$ is a symmetric monoidal category then we can construct a 2-category $\mathcal V$-Cat that consists of enriched categories, functors and natural transformations. Further we can define representable $\mathcal V$-functors and prove the Yoneda lemma.

If $\mathcal V$ is complete then $\mathcal V$-Cat is closed. This means that we can make sense of diagrams in an enriched category and define limits in enriched categories. The right type of limit for a $\mathcal V$-category is called an indexed limit. (When $\mathcal V=Set$ then indexed limits are the same as ordinary limits.) Moreover it is possible to extend $\mathcal V$ to a large 'universe' in a way that does not disturb limits and colimits. This allows us to consider functor $\mathcal V$-categories where the domain is not necessarily small.

We consider enriched category theory to be the correct setting for discussing limit-theories and completions. It is possible that the logic of symmetric monoidal categories contains the most fundamental part of classical logic.

In the same way as we can study category theory using $2$-category theory, we can study $\mathcal V$-category theory using $2$-category theory. However we do not do so in this book. It would require a treatment of weak natural transformations, which would go too far beyond the scope of the work. Therefore we also do not include:-

  • change of base induced by a symmetric monoidal functor $\mathcal V \rightarrow \mathcal V'$
  • the work of Day that constructs symmetic monoidal categories as subcategories of functor categories
  • the theory of monads and in particular the $2$-categorical treatment of monads by Street.
We also have not discussed in detail applications of enriched category theory, nor the additional results available when stronger assumptions are made about the base category.

Although we do not cover them here, there are many applications of enriched category theory. For instance:-

  • topological algebra
  • Kan extensions enriched over Banach spaces
  • enriched differential graded modules
  • finite-limit theories enriched in categories
  • change of base in the study of homotopy limits
  • infinite loop spaces
  • monoidal structures on $[G, \mathcal V]$ and cohomology groups
  • sheaves on a site are symmetric Cauchy-complete $\mathcal V$-categories.
